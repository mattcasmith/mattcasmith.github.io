SQL JOINs: INNER JOIN, LEFT JOIN, RIGHT JOIN and FULL OUTER JOIN

2018-12-21  Programming

My next post on SQL takes a look at JOINs, which allow you to compare and merge two tables in various ways and determine the relationship between two sets of data. Here‚Äôs what INNER, LEFT, RIGHT, and FULL OUTER JOINs are and how to use them.

Having gotten to grips with the construction of basic SQL queries, the next challenge that arose in my work was to merge two SQL tables based on records and values that match. In SQL, this is done with the very useful JOIN command, which has a few variants ‚Äì INNER, LEFT, RIGHT, and OUTER ‚Äì that return different combinations of records from the first, second, and both tables, and merge them differently.

To illustrate this, I‚Äôve set up two tables. The first, Positions, contains a list of team IDs and their 2017/18 Premier League finishing positions. The second, Teams, contains a list of team IDs and the teams‚Äô names. INNER JOIN

A SQL INNER JOIN returns all the records in the two tables where the values in a column match. In this case, I‚Äôm matching on the team_id column, which is the only column the two tables have in common.

In our example, this returns a list of the team IDs that match across the two tables with their corresponding names and positions. The positions that don‚Äôt have matching teams (seventh, eighth, ninth) and teams that do not have matching positions (Portsmouth, Millwall, Norwich) are excluded.

LEFT JOIN

A SQL LEFT JOIN returns all the records from your first table, and all the records that match from the second. In this example, this means we get the full list of teams, but only the positions that match.

This returns the full list of team_ids from Teams with corresponding data from Positions added where appropriate. This means Portsmouth, Millwall, and Norwich are included without positions, but the additional positions (seventh, eighth, and ninth) are left out.

A SQL RIGHT JOIN does just the opposite, returning all the records from your second table, and all the records that match from the first. This means the full list of positions will be returned.

This gives us the full list of positions from Positions, including seventh, eighth, and ninth, which have no teams. Meanwhile, Portsmouth, Millwall, and Norwich ‚Äì which have no positions ‚Äì are excluded.
